About Me

I'm a combat systems designer focused on the intersection of mechanical depth and player expression — building systems that reward mastery without punishing newcomers, and boss encounters that stay surprising across repeated runs.

My background in chemical engineering gives me a systems-thinking lens. I approach combat the same way I'd approach a reaction network: isolate the variables, define the feedback loops, iterate on parameters, and let emergent complexity arise from well-defined rules.

I work primarily in Unreal Engine 5, using modular, data-driven architectures that keep tuning fast and scope scalable. When a system needs it, I go to code — Blueprint, C#, and C++ in UE.

Decision 01
Per-Attack Data Structs Over Baked Blueprint Logic

Each boss attack bundles its animation, movement curves, starting position, and play-rate scaling into one data struct. Tuning became a single field change; adding a new attack meant filling out a struct, not touching logic.

Decision 02
Two Parallel Trees: Reactive and Probabilistic

Proactive attack selection and reactive countering competed when sharing one tree. Splitting into two parallel trees gave each its own priority — one phase-gated, one reading live player state.

Decision 03
Each Attack as a Layered Combat Puzzle

Attacks that just deal damage turn fights into memorization. Every attack in TOG imposes a constraint on position, timing, and mechanic choice — and resolves into a specific opening as the reward for reading it correctly.

Decision 04

Most boss fights peak at the attack pattern. TOG treats the defensive exchange as the fastest-paced moment of the fight — where both fighters read each other in real time — and routes every other system toward it.

Design Decisions — The Reasoning Behind the Build
Decision 01
State-Driven Camera Architecture
Early builds used hardcoded camera positions per scene. This broke every time level layout changed between milestones. I built CameraManager with a Cinemachine state machine so the level designer could trigger camera transitions through scene events — no code changes needed. This unblocked non-coders during M2 and M3 crunch.
Decision 02
World-Switch as a Physical Metaphor
The fresco theme demanded the 3D→2D shift feel like entering a painting, not loading a level. I prioritized visual continuity — flattening depth, adjusting the player controller, shifting the camera plane — so the mechanic reads as the world transforming, not the game changing mode.
Decision 03
Camera-Led Setpiece Pacing
In a short game, pacing is everything. I used camera direction — not UI or tutorial text — to guide players through major moments: wide pulls for reveals, tight frames for tension, slow pans for payoff. The approach kept the experience cinematic and author-driven without adding any onscreen clutter.

Decision 01
Per-Attack Data Structs
Over Baked Blueprint Logic
TL;DR All boss attack behavior lives in data. Adding a new enemy means new parameter assets — never new logic.
Architecture

The F_AreliusAttackParameters Struct

Early attacks had behavior scattered across Blueprint nodes — movement, starting position, velocity, animation each baked separately per attack. Tuning meant tracing through nodes; comparing two attacks was impossible.

F_AreliusAttackParameters bundles AnimMontage, MovementParameters (velocity curve), StartingPosition, PlayRate curve name, DistanceFromAttackTarget, VelocityMultiplier, and DualMontage into one struct. Attack logic reads the struct and executes — it doesn't decide what to execute.

Iteration Workflow

Tuning at Data-Asset Speed

Old: every tuning pass required a recompile. New: any parameter is a single field on a data asset — open, change, save. Minutes per adjustment became seconds, which matters when feel-tuning needs dozens of micro-iterations per attack.

Comparing two attacks became trivial: open LungeAttack and DashBackQuickThrustAttack side-by-side, line up their VelocityMultiplier, DistanceFromAttackTarget, and PlayRate values directly. Fast enough to work on instinct, not planning.

Scalability

New Enemies Without New Architecture

BTT_Attacks (dispatcher) and BTD_CheckAttackParameters (viability decorator) both read F_AreliusAttackParameters. The AI toolset is agnostic to which attack it's executing. A new enemy needs new parameter assets — no BT changes, no task or decorator changes.

The attack pool itself is data. Attacks can be added, removed, or reordered without touching code. Phase transitions swap pools at the tree level — each attack carries everything it needs to execute.

Decision 02
Two Parallel Trees:
Reactive and Probabilistic
TL;DR Reactive and proactive AI got their own trees so neither could starve the other.
Reactive Defense

BT_Arelius_DefensiveTree

In the original single-tree design, reactive counters competed with attack selection for priority. When a parry opportunity arrived, the tree had to finish its current evaluation first — and often didn't. The reaction felt slow and disconnected.

BT_Arelius_DefensiveTree runs in parallel, reading the player's AISubAction and AIReactionSubAction keys directly. Parry, block, direct hit — each routes to its own counter sequence. The reactive side has its own execution priority, separate from whatever the boss was trying to do next.

Non-Deterministic Selection

Emergent Fights via BTD_FollowUpChance

BTD_FollowUpChance is a probabilistic decorator — passes or fails on a tunable probability. When it fails, the boss doesn't follow up; it repositions, pauses, or resets. The same exchange resolves differently across runs.

Players who memorize a punish window will occasionally be wrong — not because the attack changed, but because the follow-up didn't come this time. The fight stays hard because of attention, not muscle memory.

Phase Gating

BTD_CheckPhase — Clean Pool Routing

Phase 1 has no access to projectile or teleport sub-trees — they simply don't execute when BTD_CheckPhase returns false. The gating is at the tree level, not inside the attacks: routes branch, sub-trees either run or don't.

When Phase 2 unlocks, the new attacks aren't introduced gradually — they're available immediately. The first time Arelius throws a ThrowSwordTeleport, the player's spatial model from Phase 1 breaks. The phase transition's job is to reset assumptions and force re-engagement. A boss fight that gets easier as it goes isn't designed — it's just completed.

Decision 03
Each Attack as a
Layered Combat Puzzle
TL;DR Every attack is a constraint problem with one correct opening — designed to be read, not memorized.
Attack Design

Three Constraints, One Opening

Every attack imposes three constraints: speed and range gate which player mechanics work; boss movement gates viable positioning; the opening left by a correct read is the reward for understanding both. The constraints aren't arbitrary — they're the attack's argument about where the player should be.

LungeAttack punishes a straight retreat — the answer is lateral, which also sets up a punish. DashWindUpThrust uses a long wind-up as a feint: the boss reads early parry attempts and extends the animation to catch them, training patience over reaction. PunchCounterAttack only fires when the player approaches — BTD_PlayerVelocityWithinDesiredValues reads the vector and routes to it, so the player's aggression becomes the trigger. Aggression has to be earned, not assumed.

BT_Combat_Phase1 — Core Attack Chain selector & composite sub-trees
Combo Design

Different Endings, Different Openings

Each combo ends in a different state and leaves a different opening — the correct response is context-dependent, not rote. 3AttackSeq ends in recovery lag that rewards close pressure. GapCloserAttack-CirclCombo cuts off retreat and circles — dodging away tightens the trap, dodging in breaks it. HeavyCombo is slow but staggering — the window opens only if the player holds their ground.

Mix-ups close the loop. Dodge predictably and Arelius redirects to catch the dodge; dodge the charged attack early and he extends it to punish the read — same mechanic as Sekiro's Owl. Combos aren't sequences to memorize, they're conversations. Read correctly, earn the opening; run muscle memory, get caught.

Jump Attack variation chain — follow-ups re-route by player action
Adaptive Setpiece

One Attack, Four Fights

AAA setpieces are usually single authored branches — Genichiro's mid-fight arrow volley plays the same way every time. Arelius's Phase 2 ThrowSwordTeleport is built the other way: one opening, four distinct cinematic exchanges, each triggered by how the player defended.

Hit — the blade embeds, Arelius teleports to the wound, a QTE finisher rips the sword free. Block — the thrown sword spins mid-air, the boss teleports onto its arc, slams down on the descent. Dodge — the blade freezes beside the player as a lingering threat; Arelius teleports to it and opens a full follow-up combo. Block that follow-up's opener and the sword is knocked clean from his hand — a second teleport reappears him at the disarmed blade, committed to a slam.

The seam is in animation, not logic. AN_InitiateTeleportToSword fires from inside each branch's montage, stitching the exchange into one moment — no BT re-evaluation, no idle recovery. The player reads one cinematic attack; the system plays whichever of the four they earned. The scene the player sees is the scene they caused.

This breaks TOG's standard authoring pattern. Every other attack runs through a generalized system — fill out the data struct, arrange the BT, the shared runtime executes. The four branches here needed bespoke event graphs per branch: teleport staging, sword visibility, projectile end-state, per-outcome reentry. The setpiece earned its own code.

Pacing & Flow State

Designing the Entry and the Exit

Flow state is the moment the player stops deliberating and starts reading — when reaction becomes intuition and the fight feels like rhythm. Every attack builds toward this: telegraphed paths (golden threads lighting the weapon before a swing) create the foundation; mix-ups and phase transitions break it deliberately. The question isn't whether the player enters flow — it's how fast they get there and what it feels like to be knocked out.

Phase 1 builds the model. Arelius stays grounded, melee-range, learnable. Phase 2 introduces ThrowSwordTeleport and VanishingStep — the spatial model breaks, flow is disrupted. The reset: remove what the player was relying on, then give them space to rebuild. The design question isn't "is this fair" — it's "how long until they find their footing, and does getting there feel earned?"

Decision 04
The Defensive Tree
as the Climax
TL;DR The defensive exchange is the fight's peak — every other AI system hands off into it.
Design Intent

The Fight's Peak Is a Two-Way Conversation

Most boss fights crescendo on the boss's turn — the unblockable, the cinematic sweep, the moment the player is reduced to reading one cue. TOG inverts it: the crescendo is the exchange itself. The defensive tree is where both fighters are reading each other simultaneously — and it runs at the fastest pace in the fight.

Every other AI system routes toward this moment. Probabilistic selection sets up the pressure that earns an opening. Phase gating decides what tools are in play when the opening arrives. Combo branching decides which exchange the opening becomes. All of it hands off into the defensive tree — the one place the fight is actually being decided.

Counter-Attack Logic

Hit-and-Run Gets Caught

Two player behaviors, two answers. Land a single hit and disengage — the boss counter-attacks immediately, punishing the retreat before the player resets to neutral. Commit to sustained pressure — the boss absorbs the first hits, then perfect-parries at a threshold and opens its own close-range defensive combo, taking back the exchange from inside it.

The asymmetry is the point. Hit-and-run is normally safe against a reactive boss; TOG makes it the punished option. Sustained aggression is normally reckless; TOG makes it viable, if you survive the perfect-parry it provokes. Breaking through means reading which counter is being set up — direct counter means the player didn't commit, perfect-parry means they over-committed. Neither is free.

BT_Arelius_DefensiveTree — Counter Attack branch & Reset Logic
Pace Reset

Break-Away as Rhythm Control

Inside an extended defensive exchange, the boss occasionally breaks momentum and creates space — backward dash, jump-back, QuickFarRangeDash — then re-engages on a new angle. These aren't escapes. They're rhythm resets. Without them, every defensive pass settles into the same tempo. With them, each pass has its own shape, and the re-engage becomes the moment the player has to re-read what just happened.

The break-away sub-tree runs under the defensive logic, gated by exchange duration and the boss's state. Long enough exchange, low stamina, right player velocity — the break-away fires and the fight breathes for half a second before the next trade. The reset isn't retreat; it's how a fast loop avoids feeling like a single-note sustain.

Break-Away sub-tree — distance creation & re-engage routing
Tree Transitions

Handing Off the Fight

Offensive and defensive trees run in parallel, but execution is always in one at a time. The handoff is player-driven: a parry, a clean hit, or an action that breaks the boss's current attack flips the AISubAction key and the defensive tree takes priority mid-evaluation. The boss doesn't wait for its combo to finish recovering — it answers while the player's input is still live.

The player always feels like their action caused the transition. Nothing waits on a cooldown; nothing waits for an animation to finish. Hit lands → defensive tree picks up → counter or parry fires on the correct frame. The fight never stalls because the handoff is the player's input, not the AI's decision.

Boss AI Architecture — BPC_Attacks_TOG
Dynamic Runtime
Behavior

Attacks and combos are organized into branches within the combat tree. Every time an attack doesn't fire, it accumulates points — pressure building toward inevitability. Selection chance is its current points divided by the branch total: a live probability distribution that shifts across the entire fight based on what actually works against this player.

// Selection Probability
P(i) = Pi Σ nj=1 Pj
// Point Accumulation (attack i not selected at decision)
Pi ← Pi + 1 · mi · w(Hi)
base = 1  ·  mi ∈ [0.7, 1.3]  ·  Pi(init) = 0
// On Successful Hit
mi ← clamp(mi + Δm,  0.7,  1.3)
Pi ← 0
// On Miss or Block
mi ← clamp(mi − Δm,  0.7,  1.3)
// On Perfect Parry
mi unchanged
Pi — accumulated points for attack i; initialized at 0, updated only on attack result
n — total attacks in the branch
mi — effectiveness modifier; clamped to [0.7, 1.3]; rises on hit, falls on miss/block, untouched on perfect parry
w(Hi) — hit weight; scales points added based on whether the attack would connect
Pi ← 0 — reset on successful hit; enforces cooldown before the attack can re-accumulate

Points only move when an attack result is evaluated — not on a timer. A perfect parry leaves the modifier untouched: the player executed at the highest level, and the system doesn't penalize the boss for it. Blocks and misses pull the modifier down, so attacks that aren't connecting accumulate slower and naturally fall back in the distribution.

The system only runs on the combat tree. Narrative attacks — phase openers and crescendo moments that define the fight's emotional arc — are hard-coded to fire reliably. The defensive tree carries no point system at all: those exchanges need to feel like the player is barely surviving, and a weighted distribution would undercut that pressure.

Where the system has the most impact is on ranged attacks and gap-closers. As the distribution shifts, the fight's rhythm changes — trading close-quarters pressure for a cat-and-mouse dynamic of weaving through ranged attacks to find an opening on an unguarded enemy. The same boss, a different fight.
