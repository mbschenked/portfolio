About Me

I'm a combat systems designer focused on the intersection of mechanical depth and player expression — building systems that reward mastery without punishing newcomers, and boss encounters that stay surprising across repeated runs.

My background in chemical engineering gives me a systems-thinking lens. I approach combat the same way I'd approach a reaction network: isolate the variables, define the feedback loops, iterate on parameters, and let emergent complexity arise from well-defined rules.

I work primarily in Unreal Engine 5, using modular, data-driven architectures that keep tuning fast and scope scalable. When a system needs it, I go to code — Blueprint, C#, and C++ in UE.

Design Decisions — The Reasoning Behind the Build
Decision 01
Per-Attack Data Structs Over Baked Blueprint Logic

Each boss attack bundles its animation, movement curves, starting position, and play-rate scaling into one data struct. Tuning became a single field change; adding a new attack meant filling out a struct, not touching logic.

Decision 02
Two Parallel Trees: Reactive and Probabilistic

Proactive attack selection and reactive countering competed when sharing one tree. Splitting into two parallel trees gave each its own priority — one phase-gated, one reading live player state.

Decision 03
Each Attack as a Layered Combat Puzzle

Attacks that just deal damage turn fights into memorization. Every attack in TOG imposes a constraint on position, timing, and mechanic choice — and resolves into a specific opening as the reward for reading it correctly.

Design Decisions — The Reasoning Behind the Build
Decision 01
State-Driven Camera Architecture
Early builds used hardcoded camera positions per scene. This broke every time level layout changed between milestones. I built CameraManager with a Cinemachine state machine so the level designer could trigger camera transitions through scene events — no code changes needed. This unblocked non-coders during M2 and M3 crunch.
Decision 02
World-Switch as a Physical Metaphor
The fresco theme demanded the 3D→2D shift feel like entering a painting, not loading a level. I prioritized visual continuity — flattening depth, adjusting the player controller, shifting the camera plane — so the mechanic reads as the world transforming, not the game changing mode.
Decision 03
Camera-Led Setpiece Pacing
In a short game, pacing is everything. I used camera direction — not UI or tutorial text — to guide players through major moments: wide pulls for reveals, tight frames for tension, slow pans for payoff. The approach kept the experience cinematic and author-driven without adding any onscreen clutter.

Decision 01
Per-Attack Data Structs
Over Baked Blueprint Logic
Architecture

The F_AreliusAttackParameters Struct

Early attack implementations had behavior scattered across Blueprint nodes — movement speed, starting position, velocity multipliers, and animation all baked in separately per attack. Changing one parameter meant tracing through nodes; cross-attack comparison was impossible.

I centralized everything into F_AreliusAttackParameters — a single struct bundling AnimMontage, MovementParameters (velocity curve), StartingPosition, AttackSpecificPlayRateCurveName, ScaleAttackVelocityWithPlayRate, DistanceFromAttackTarget, VelocityMultiplier, and DualMontage. Attack logic reads the struct and executes. It doesn't decide what to execute. Behavior lives entirely in data.

Iteration Workflow

Tuning at Data-Asset Speed

With parameters baked into nodes, every tuning pass required a recompile. With the struct, any parameter is a single field in a data asset — open, change, save. The loop dropped from minutes to seconds per adjustment, which matters when feel-based tuning requires dozens of micro-iterations per attack.

Comparing two attacks side-by-side became trivial. LungeAttack and DashBackQuickThrustAttack could be opened simultaneously — their VelocityMultiplier, DistanceFromAttackTarget, and PlayRate values compared directly. This made the tuning process fast enough to work on instinct rather than planning.

Scalability

New Enemies Without New Architecture

BTT_Attacks (the main dispatcher) and BTD_CheckAttackParameters (the viability decorator) both read F_AreliusAttackParameters. The entire AI toolset is agnostic to which attack it's executing — it reads the struct and acts. A future enemy only needs a new set of parameter assets. No changes to the behavior tree, no changes to the task or decorator logic.

The attack pool itself is data. Attacks can be added, removed, or reordered without touching code. Phase transitions swap pools by routing the BT at the tree level — the attacks themselves carry everything needed to execute.

Decision 02
Two Parallel Trees:
Reactive and Probabilistic
Reactive Defense

BT_Arelius_DefensiveTree

In the original single-tree design, reactive counters competed with attack selection for execution priority. When a parry opportunity was detected, the tree had to finish its current evaluation before responding — and often didn't. The reaction felt slow and disconnected from what the player actually did.

BT_Arelius_DefensiveTree runs as a parallel tree, reading the player's AISubAction and AIReactionSubAction blackboard keys directly. A parry triggers a specific counter sequence chosen for that exact situation. A blocked hit triggers a different one. A direct hit on the boss triggers another. The boss's reactive side now has its own execution priority, completely separate from what the boss was trying to do next.

Non-Deterministic Selection

Emergent Fights via BTD_FollowUpChance

BTD_FollowUpChance is a probabilistic decorator — it passes or fails based on a tunable probability value. When it fails, the boss doesn't follow up. It repositions, pauses, or resets instead. The same attack sequence can resolve differently across different runs of the same exchange.

Players who memorize a punish window will occasionally be wrong — not because the attack changed, but because the follow-up didn't come this time. This keeps the encounter from fully normalizing regardless of how many attempts the player has made. The fight stays hard because of player attention, not just accumulated muscle memory.

Phase Gating

BTD_CheckPhase — Clean Pool Routing

Phase 1 has no access to projectile attacks or teleport sequences — those sub-trees simply don't execute when BTD_CheckPhase returns false. No conditional logic lives inside the attack nodes themselves. The gating is entirely at the tree level: routes branch, sub-trees either run or don't.

When Phase 2 unlocks, the new attacks aren't introduced gradually — they're available immediately. The first time Arelius throws a ThrowSwordTeleport, the player's spatial model from Phase 1 breaks. This is deliberate: the phase transition's job is to reset the player's assumptions and force re-engagement. A boss fight that gets easier as it goes isn't designed — it's just completed.

Decision 03
Each Attack as a
Layered Combat Puzzle
Attack Design

Three Constraints, One Opening

Every attack was designed against three constraints: speed and range define which player mechanics are viable; the boss's movement during the attack defines viable positioning; and the specific opening left by a correct read is the reward for understanding both. The constraints aren't arbitrary — they're the attack's argument about where the player should be and what they should do next.

LungeAttack punishes retreating straight back — the answer is lateral, which also positions the player for a punish. DashWindUpThrust uses a long wind-up as a feint: the boss reads early parry attempts and extends the animation to catch reflex parries, training patience over reaction. PunchCounterAttack only fires when the player approaches — BTD_PlayerVelocityWithinDesiredValues detects the approach vector and routes to the counter, meaning the player's own aggression becomes the trigger. Aggression has to be earned, not assumed.

Combo Design

Different Endings, Different Openings

Each combo sequence ends in a different state and leaves a different opening — the player's correct response to a resolved combo should always be context-dependent, never rote. A resolved 3AttackSeq ends in recovery lag that rewards close pressure. GapCloserAttack-CirclCombo cuts off retreat and circles — dodging away tightens the trap, dodging into the combo breaks it. HeavyCombo is slow but staggering; the window is generous but only opens if the player holds their ground through the intimidation.

Mix-ups close the loop. If the player starts dodging predictably, the boss redirects its follow-up to catch the dodge. Dodge the charged attack too early and Arelius extends it to punish the read — the same mechanic Sekiro's Owl uses. Combos aren't sequences to memorize. They're conversations that branch on the player's response. The player who reads correctly earns the opening; the player running muscle memory gets caught.

Pacing & Flow State

Engineering the Entry and the Exit

Flow state in a combat puzzle game is the moment the player stops deliberating and starts reading — where reaction becomes intuition and the fight feels like a rhythm rather than a problem. Every attack in TOG builds toward this: telegraphed attack paths (golden threads lighting the weapon before a swing) create the foundation; mix-ups and phase transitions break it deliberately. The question isn't whether the player will enter flow — it's how fast they get there, and what it feels like when they're knocked out of it.

Phase 1 builds the model. Arelius stays grounded, attacks are melee-range, the rhythm is learnable. The goal is to get the player into flow on those terms. Phase 2 introduces ThrowSwordTeleport and VanishingStep — the spatial model breaks, flow is disrupted. This is the reset: remove the one thing the player was relying on, then give them the space to rebuild. The design question for every disruption isn't "is this fair" — it's "how long does it take the player to find their footing again, and does getting there feel earned?"
